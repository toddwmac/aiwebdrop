The Mounting Burden of Federal Compliance on Small Universities

How recent Department of Education regulations and broader federal policies are reshaping the operational landscape for higher education institutions

Key Compliance Areas

  • Accreditation Reforms
  • Title IX Expansion
  • Financial Aid Oversight
  • Gainful Employment Rules

Executive Summary

Federal activities in recent months have ushered in a period of significant change and increased regulatory scrutiny for higher education institutions, with small universities facing distinct challenges due to their typically more limited resources.

Key Developments

  • Sweeping reforms to the accreditation system aimed at increasing institutional flexibility but also imposing new requirements related to student outcomes and intellectual diversity
  • Enhanced financial aid oversight with new fraud prevention measures and reporting requirements
  • Expanded Title IX regulations broadening protections but also lowering thresholds for institutional response
  • Gainful Employment rules demanding meticulous data collection and reporting on program-level student outcomes

Cumulatively, these activities translate into increased administrative and IT system adjustments, extensive staff training, policy revisions, and complex resource allocation and strategic planning challenges for small universities, which must also prepare for potential future legislative changes and evolving enforcement landscapes.

Direct Impacts: New Department of Education Regulations

Accreditation Reforms and Institutional Flexibility

Recent federal activities, particularly an Executive Order issued on April 23, 2025, titled "Reforming Accreditation to Strengthen Higher Education," signal significant shifts in the landscape of higher education accreditation. [30]

Key Changes

  • Reduced barriers to changing accreditors
  • Emphasis on "intellectual diversity" among faculty
  • De-emphasis on DEI mandates
  • Focus on high-quality academic programs

Workload Implications

  • Review and revise DEI policies
  • Research new accreditor options
  • Update data collection processes
  • Navigate transition complexities
"The Executive Order asserts that accreditors have, in some instances, 'abused their authority by imposing discriminatory diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI)-based standards' and have approved 'low-quality' institutions, leading to poor student outcomes and significant debt."

Enhanced Financial Aid Oversight and Fraud Prevention

The Department of Education has intensified its focus on the integrity of federal student aid programs, announcing several measures aimed at preventing fraud, particularly identity theft, and ensuring that aid reaches eligible students. [8]

Identity Verification

New V4 and V5 verification categories for fall 2025

NSLDS Reports

New Delinquent Borrower Report (DELQ01)

Foreign Gifts

Section 117 reporting deadline July 31, 2025

For small universities, these changes translate into an increased administrative burden in the financial aid office, requiring staff training on new verification procedures, system updates for XML reporting formats, and enhanced monitoring of foreign gift reporting requirements.

Gainful Employment Rule: Ongoing Compliance

The U.S. Department of Education's Gainful Employment (GE) regulations, effective July 1, 2024, represent a significant and ongoing compliance obligation for both for-profit and not-for-profit institutions. [3]

Critical Metrics

Debt-to-Earnings Ratio

Program passes if debt payments are:

  • ≤ 8% of annual earnings OR
  • ≤ 20% of discretionary earnings
Earnings Premium Test

Graduate earnings must exceed typical high school graduate in state labor force (~$25,000 average)

Institutions must annually report 23 distinct data elements for each program, including program length, total enrollment, costs, loan debt, and earnings data. The administrative burden of collecting, verifying, and reporting this data is substantial, particularly for smaller institutions with limited administrative staff and resources.

Expanded Title IX Regulations

The Department of Education's new Title IX regulations, which took effect on August 1, 2024, represent a significant expansion of institutional responsibilities and are poised to substantially increase the compliance workload for all higher education institutions. [103]

Expanded Protections

  • Sexual orientation discrimination
  • Gender identity discrimination
  • Pregnancy and related conditions
  • Parental, family, marital status

Lower Thresholds

  • From "severe, pervasive and objectively offensive"
  • To "severe or pervasive" and objectively offensive

Legal Challenges

As of August 2024, multiple lawsuits challenging certain provisions have resulted in preliminary injunctions barring implementation in 26 states, creating a patchwork of compliance requirements across the country. [106]

Indirect Impacts: Broader Federal Policies

Office for Civil Rights (OCR) Enforcement Priorities

Recent activities by the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) indicate a significant shift in enforcement priorities and caseload management. A POLITICO report revealed that the OCR has been dismissing civil rights complaints at an "unheard of" pace since March 2025. [81]

The agency appears to be increasingly focusing its civil rights efforts on stamping out campus antisemitism, potentially altering the compliance landscape for institutions. This creates uncertainty for small universities, which must navigate shifting enforcement priorities with limited legal and administrative resources.

Immigration Policies and International Student Dynamics

Recent U.S. immigration policies and enforcement actions have created a complex and often challenging environment for international students and the universities that host them. A June 2025 travel ban proclamation included a "total ban" on citizens from 12 countries entering the United States. [97]

Travel Ban Impact

F, M, and J student visas banned for:

Total Ban: Afghanistan, Burma, Chad, Congo, Equatorial Guinea, Eritrea, Haiti, Iran, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Yemen

Partial Ban: Burundi, Cuba, Laos, Sierra Leone, Togo, Turkmenistan, Venezuela

Enforcement Actions

  • Arrests of protest participants
  • Visa cancellations for op-ed writers
  • SEVIS terminations without notice
  • Climate of fear on campuses
"Some universities are reportedly checking on visa revocations hourly and being on call to quickly inform affected students of SEVIS terminations." [98]

OMB Directives and Funding Uncertainties

Recent actions by the White House and the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) have introduced significant funding uncertainties for higher education institutions. In January 2025, the OMB issued a memorandum instructing federal agencies to temporarily halt all spending while reviewing funding for alignment with administration priorities. [107]

Critical Impact

Funding for programs like the College Assistance Migrant Program (CAMP) and High School Equivalency Program (HEP) was withheld in July 2025, forcing some institutions to consider closing programs and laying off staff. [110]

The OMB memo specifically targeted discretionary grants at the Department of Education for review concerning alignment with administration priorities, including DEI initiatives and "woke gender ideology". This direct impact on specific programs demonstrates how broader OMB reviews can translate into immediate operational crises for universities.

Case Study: Bowdoin College and OCR

The Complaint

On May 21, 2025, the Equal Protection Project (EPP) filed a federal civil rights complaint against Bowdoin College with the OCR, alleging that the college administers eighteen distinct scholarships and programs that discriminate on the basis of race, color, national origin, and/or sex. [89]

Examples of Challenged Scholarships:

  • Alice G. and Frederick W. Titus Scholarship Fund: Preference to "students from minority groups who enhance the diversity of campus"
  • Sweeney Family Scholarship Fund: Preference to "female students who have an interest in technology"

Workload Impact

  • Internal investigation
  • Document gathering and submission
  • Legal counsel engagement
  • Policy revision and training
  • Media relations and reputation management

The EPP's complaint requests that OCR promptly open a formal investigation, impose remedial relief for those allegedly excluded from scholarships, and ensure future compliance with federal civil rights laws. For a small university like Bowdoin, responding to such a complaint entails considerable administrative and legal workload, even before any formal finding of wrongdoing.

Potential Future Implications

Legislative Proposals

The U.S. Senate's HELP Committee proposals for a Reconciliation bill in June 2025 aim to "fix our broken higher education system" by prioritizing student success and 21st-century skills. [1]

Key Proposals

  • • Elimination of Grad PLUS loans
  • • Caps on unsubsidized graduate loans
  • • Workforce Pell Grants for short-term programs
  • • "Do no harm" standard for program eligibility

Potential Impact

  • • Reduced loan availability for students
  • • Increased pressure on institutional aid
  • • Competition with non-accredited providers
  • • Enhanced outcome tracking requirements

Evolving Enforcement Landscapes

Both OCR and accrediting agencies are expected to continue evolving their enforcement approaches, presenting ongoing compliance challenges for small universities.

OCR Focus Areas

  • Dynamic enforcement priorities
  • Campus antisemitism investigations
  • DEI program scrutiny
  • Transgender rights interpretations

Accreditation Changes

  • Greater institutional flexibility
  • Student outcome emphasis
  • DEI mandate de-emphasis
  • New accreditor competition

Broader Policy Shifts

National debates surrounding DEI programs and immigration policies will continue to indirectly impact small universities, creating ongoing compliance challenges.

DEI Challenges

Continued legal challenges, OCR investigations, and pressure to modify or justify DEI efforts

Examples: Policy review, legal defense, community engagement

Immigration Impact

Ongoing travel restrictions, visa scrutiny, and campus climate concerns

Examples: SEVIS compliance, student support, advocacy efforts

Conclusion and Outlook

The past six months have demonstrated a significant intensification of federal activities impacting higher education, with small universities facing a particularly challenging environment due to their typically more constrained resources.

Current Challenges

  • Direct impacts from new Department of Education regulations
  • Indirect pressures from evolving OCR enforcement
  • Restrictive immigration policies affecting international students
  • Funding uncertainties from OMB directives

Required Actions

  • Enhance compliance infrastructure
  • Invest in data management capabilities
  • Foster cross-department collaboration
  • Maintain strategic agility
"The outlook is one of continued adaptation and resilience, as these institutions strive to uphold their educational missions while meeting the demands of an increasingly complex federal regulatory environment."

Small universities must prepare for potential future implications, including new legislative proposals that could reshape federal student aid and institutional accountability, an ever-evolving enforcement landscape, and the continued ripple effects of broader policy shifts concerning DEI and immigration. Proactive engagement with federal policy developments, robust internal controls, and a clear articulation of institutional mission and values will be crucial for small universities to not only manage the increased workload but also to thrive in this era of heightened federal scrutiny and regulatory change.
